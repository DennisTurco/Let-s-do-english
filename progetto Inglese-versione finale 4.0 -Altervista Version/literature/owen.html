Wilfred Owen

Life

- 1893 Wilfred Owen was born in Shropshire, England
- 1915 He was still in France when World War I broke out. He returned to England, enlisted in the British Army and sailed to France.
- 1917 In May an exploding shell left him psychologically traumatisedand he was sent back to Britain to recover at Craiglockart War Hospital.
- 1918 Owen returned with his regiment to France and was awarded the Military Cross for bravery at the Battle of Amiens, but tragically he was killed on 4 November.

Dulce et Decorum Est

This poem, written while Owen was at Craiglockhart War Hospital in 1917, is a passionate denunciation of patriotic encouragement to young man to enlist and die for their country.


Analysis

The poem is a combination of two sonets: it is divided into 4 stanzas.
In the frist stanza Owen is speaking in first singular person, putting himself with his fellow soldiers as they are retreating towards the trenches. The tone is slow, it describes weary movements due to physical exhaustation.
In the second stanza there's the description of a gas attack; the tone is convulsive because of the panic, in fact one of the soldiers is about to die due to "the thick green light".
In the third stanza Owen dreams the drowning soldier.
And in the fourth stanza he criticize those who promulgate going to war as a glorious endeevor: the poet describes his friends' horrible death from chemical warfare and bitterly conveys the message of the poem.

Craiglockhart War Hospital for Officers

War caused numbers of injuries and often special hospitals had to be set up to deal with them.
In 1916, during the World War I, Craiglockhart Hospital for officers was set up near Edinburgh, to deal with shell-shocked officers.
Patients were kept active and there were sporting activities including swimming, golf, tennis and cricket, or walking in the fields around the hospital.
The patients would be occupied during the day, but often wandered the corridors at night. Many of them were haunted by the horrible scenes of death and destruction that they had experienced.
Craiglockhart's most famous patients were the war poets Siegfried Sassoon and Wilfred Owen.
